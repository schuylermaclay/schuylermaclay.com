A Note on Names: “Town Hall,” “Town Meeting,” and What They Mean in Vermont

Before following the history of Warren’s public buildings, it’s worth pausing on two phrases that get used loosely — including in this document — in ways that can obscure as much as they clarify: town hall and town meeting.

Town meeting is the event: Vermont’s form of direct democracy, practiced since Bennington’s first meeting in 1762. It is the annual assembly at which legal voters gather to vote on the budget, elect officers, and act on the warning articles. Vermont is one of the last places on earth where face-to-face, binding democratic assembly is the actual mechanism of government — not a consultative gathering, not a formality. Citizens speak for themselves, debate each article, and vote by voice or show of hands. No representatives, no intermediaries.

Town hall, in Vermont usage, carries two meanings that are easy to conflate. It can refer to a specific building — a dedicated structure for meetings, offices, and community events. Warren built such a building in 1872, and it is still called the “Town Hall.” But “town hall” also gets used generically to mean wherever the meeting is currently held, even when that’s a school gymnasium. In Warren today, the floor vote of Town Meeting takes place at Warren Elementary School — not in the Town Hall building at 413 Main Street, which now houses the library.

This split is not unusual in Vermont. Warren’s earliest town meetings were held in private homes. Later they moved to the Red School House in East Warren, then to the Brick Schoolhouse in the Village, and only after 1872 to the dedicated Town Hall. Even after that, the Select Board’s regular meetings have been held at the Municipal Building on Cemetery Road — not at the Town Hall itself. The two buildings serve different civic functions, though both carry civic weight.

Town Hall · Town Meeting · Municipal Building — Keeping Them Straight in Warren
  • Town Hall (the building) — 413 Main Street. Built 1872. Now houses the library. Historic stage, sprung dance floor upstairs.
  • Municipal Building — 42 Cemetery Road. Originally the 1829 Village Schoolhouse. Now houses Town Clerk, Town Administrator, Board of Listers. Select Board meets here.
  • Town Meeting (the event) — Annual floor vote, first Tuesday in March, held at Warren Elementary School, not at the Town Hall building.
  • Note — The town’s own website currently describes the library as “next to” the Municipal Building — a description left over from before the 2009 move, or reflecting that the buildings are physically adjacent on Cemetery Road/Main Street.

The naming confusion matters for understanding Article 4. When voters were asked “Shall the Town vote to make the Town Hall the permanent home for the library?” they were being asked about the building at 413 Main Street — not about where town meeting is held. The library had been there since 2009. The vote was about making that temporary arrangement permanent.

Town Meeting Day, March 3, 2026

At 5:00 in the afternoon on Tuesday, March 3, 2026, Warren’s legal voters gathered at Warren Elementary School on School Road — as they have every March for more than two centuries — to act on the town’s annual warning. This year’s agenda ran to eleven articles and included routine matters like the tax rate and the PTO allocation, along with a question that had been building for seventeen years.

The meeting was recorded by MRVTV (Mad River Valley Television) and is publicly archived at mrvtv.com for anyone who wishes to watch the proceedings. Minutes are typically drafted and posted within several weeks by the Town Clerk at warrenvt.org.

As is Warren’s tradition, articles 1 through 9 were debated and voted from the floor by voice and hand. Australian ballot voting for elected officers ran from 7:00 AM to 7:00 PM at the same location.

Article 4’s wording — “make the Town Hall the permanent home for the library” — is an advisory question voted from the floor. It does not itself appropriate funds or mandate any action. What it provides is a clearer directive to the Select Board about where the library should be housed as both bodies plan for the future.

Article 4 passed. The full floor discussion is available in the MRVTV recording at mrvtv.com/1008801-2/. Official minutes will be posted by the Town Clerk at warrenvt.org. The Valley Reporter, the valley’s paper of record, covered the meeting in its March 5, 2026 edition (subscription required).

The Question Behind the Vote

Article 4 did not emerge from nowhere. It is the crystallized form of a conversation Warren has been having — in select board minutes, library trustees’ reports, and town meeting halls — for the better part of two decades.

In 2007, voters approved $200,000 for renovation of the Town Hall (then sometimes called the Opera House) and simultaneously approved a planning process to find a site for a permanent, standalone library building. Two years later, the library completed a “temporary” move into the renovated Town Hall. The permanent building was never built — interrupted by the 2008 financial crisis, among other factors — and seventeen years on, the library remains in what was always called a temporary arrangement.

“The community built the shelving by hand — all made by Vermont woodworkers — and moved the collection book by book in a bucket brigade.”
Warren Library history, describing the 2009 move to Town Hall

Over those seventeen years, the library has grown into the space. The historic stage was preserved as the children’s reading area. The building fund — accumulating at roughly $5,000 per year — has reached approximately $82,503. But the governance relationship has not always been smooth. Vermont law (22 V.S.A. § 143, strengthened by Act 150 of 2024) grants library trustees independent authority over their budget, staff, and operations — a structural tension with any select board that also controls the building the library operates in.

One Day Before the Vote

On March 2, 2026 — the day before town meeting — the Select Board signed a $6,000 contract with Wiemann Lamphere Architects for space planning at the Municipal Building at 42 Cemetery Road: a study of how staff might be reorganized within the existing building. The contract appeared in the March 10 Select Board packet. Whatever voters decided about the library, the town was already beginning to think carefully about its other facility.

Town Budget vs. Library Building Fund — 2026

The Library Building Improvement Fund — established to accumulate toward a permanent library structure — holds approximately $82,503, or 1.5% of the total town budget of $5,411,053. It adds roughly $5,000 per year. Sources: 2026 Town Warning; Warren Town Report 2025.

A History of Warren’s Public Buildings

To understand why Warren is debating where to put its library, it helps to understand where every previous generation put its civic institutions — and how those choices have rippled forward across 225 years.

September 20, 1798
First Town Meeting — Private Homes
The first town meeting is held. Samuel Lard is elected the first town clerk. Selectmen include Ruel Sherman, Joseph Raymond, and Seth Leavitt. Location: private homes of the selectmen, which remain the venue for official meetings through 1812.
c. 1800 — 1812
Freeman’s Meetings at Private Homes
The first Freeman’s Meeting is held September 2, 1800, with twelve men taking the Freeman’s Oath. The town’s business continues to be conducted at the homes of selectmen — a common practice in early Vermont towns before purpose-built civic structures existed. The geographic center of activity was East Warren, where the best farmland lay and where the earliest settlement had taken hold.
1812 — c. 1830
Red School House, East Warren
For eighteen years, town meetings move to the Red School House in East Warren. This coincides with the period when East Warren remained the commercial and civic center. Meanwhile, settlement along the Mad River is accelerating: dams and mills appear, the Mad River Turnpike (now Rt. 100) is surveyed in 1807, and the population shifts westward toward the Village.
1823 (organized) / 1829 (built)
Village School House (Brick) — South Side of the Common
The Village School is organized in 1823 and built in brick in 1829 on the south side of the Village Common. Its construction marks the formal establishment of the village as the center of civic life. After 1840, Freeman’s Meetings shift entirely to the Village. The brick schoolhouse hosts both education and government for the next several decades — a dual use that would become a recurring pattern in Warren’s civic history.
1838–1839
Warren River Meeting House (Church)
The church/meetinghouse is built by the Free Will Baptists, Universalists, Congregationalists, and Methodists jointly. Large public assemblies increasingly have a dedicated civic-religious space, reducing the schoolhouse’s role as the primary gathering point. For decades, church ladies serve meals at the noon recess of Town Meeting.
c. 1840 — 1872
Brick Schoolhouse — All Town Meetings Held Here
After 1840, Warren holds all Freeman’s Meetings in the Village, at the Brick Schoolhouse. By the 1850s–1860s, the town’s population has grown significantly, the mill economy is near its peak (by 1870 there are as many as nine mills along the Mad River), and the schoolhouse is straining under the weight of both educational and civic demands. By the late 1860s, it is clear a dedicated town hall is needed.
1872
Warren Town Hall — 413 Main Street
“By 1872, the population had grown so that neither the school nor the larger homes could accommodate the meetings, and it was voted to build a town hall.” The land — just enough for the building — was donated by Ed Cardell. At the first meeting in the new building, the Freeman’s Meetings were formally dissolved as a category. The building features a sprung dance floor on the upper level and a stage. A basement was added sometime in the 1950s. The 2007–2009 renovation adapted the building for library use. Town meetings were formally held here from 1872; today, the annual floor vote has moved to Warren Elementary School while the Town Hall building itself houses the library.
1900
Warren Public Library — Founded by Town Meeting Vote
The library is established by a vote of Town Meeting, beginning a formal collection. Its early decades are spent in various arrangements — the record notes that when it eventually moved to the Municipal Building in 1974, it had grown to 1,000 catalogued books. The specific location of the library in its earliest decades (1900–1974) is not clearly documented in available sources; the 1974 move to the Municipal Building is the first clearly recorded permanent home.
1947
Warren Volunteer Fire Department — North End of Village
The volunteer fire department is organized under the leadership of Clayton Neill, its first chief, in 1947. Its first quarters are at the north end of the Village. The present fire station — built into the bank at the entrance of the Common — comes later; an addition was dedicated October 23, 1977, in honor of John Snow, who served as fire chief for twenty-two years.
1952
Village School House Remodeled — 42 Cemetery Road
The original brick Village Schoolhouse (built 1829, rebuilt in frame c. 1867) is significantly remodeled: a third room is added, a well is drilled, and a heating plant is installed. By 1960, it is the only school still operating in Warren. The growth of the recreation industry soon renders even this school too small, prompting the construction of Warren Elementary School on the Brooks Field Recreation Area. The old school building transitions to administrative uses.
1974
Library Moves to Municipal Building — 42 Cemetery Road
“The Village School was turned into offices for public officials and space for the library which had been started in 1900. The move to the Municipal Building (formerly the Village School House) took place in 1974.” With 1,000 books catalogued by librarian Lois Kaufmann and volunteers, the library finds its first formal permanent home inside what is now called the Municipal Building — the same building that houses the Town Clerk and other offices to this day. This is the first time the library and town offices explicitly share a building.
2007
Town Meeting Votes $200K for Town Hall Renovation — and a New Library
Voters approve a $200,000 renovation of the Town Hall at 413 Main Street and a community planning process to find a site for a standalone permanent library. The intent is for the library to eventually have its own facility. Architect Ellen Strauss is engaged by library trustees for schematic designs; engineering reports confirm floor reinforcement is needed for book stacks. The Town opens renovation bids and pursues historic preservation grants and CDBG funding.
2009
Library Moves (Temporarily) to Town Hall — 413 Main Street
Following the Town Hall renovation, the library relocates from the Municipal Building to the renovated Town Hall. Vermont woodworkers build all shelving. The community moves the collection in a “bucket brigade.” The historic stage is preserved as the children’s area. The move is explicitly described as temporary. The permanent building is never constructed — the 2008 financial crisis is among the factors that halt planning — and the library remains in the Town Hall to the present.
March 3, 2026
Article 4 Passes: Town Hall Is the Permanent Library Home
Seventeen years after the “temporary” move, Warren voters settle the question from the floor. The Select Board had signed a $6,000 contract with Wiemann Lamphere Architects the day before — for space planning at the Municipal Building, the former library’s home from 1974–2009. Both buildings’ futures remain in motion. Full meeting recorded by MRVTV: mrvtv.com/1008801-2/

Warren currently operates three primary civic facilities, all within a few hundred feet of each other in the Village center. A fourth — the fire station — is also nearby. All appear within the Warren Village Historic District, listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1992.

Vermont Context: Libraries, Town Halls, and Who Controls What

Warren’s question is not unique in Vermont. The state has dozens of small towns where the library and the municipal offices share space — and where the governance relationship between an independent board of library trustees and an elected select board creates structural tension.

Vermont Library Trustee Independence (22 V.S.A. § 143)

Vermont law grants library trustees independent authority to set library budgets, hire the library director, and govern library operations — subject only to a voter-approved appropriation. Act 150 of 2024 strengthened these provisions. This means that a library operating within a town-owned building is simultaneously under the authority of the select board (as landlord) and under the independent governance of its own trustees (as operator). In Warren, this structural ambiguity has surfaced periodically in select board discussions about rental policy, budget modifications, and staffing decisions.

Vermont Municipal Bond Bank current rate environment: comparable small-town bonds have run 1.98–3.5% over 15–20 years.

Moretown: The Nearest Neighbor

Moretown, Warren’s immediate neighbor down the Mad River Valley, operates its Memorial Library inside the Town Hall — making it the most directly comparable arrangement to Warren’s current situation. This model has operated there for years without the formal governance tension that has surfaced in Warren, in part because Moretown’s circumstances differ. But it represents one endpoint of the spectrum: library fully embedded in municipal facility, permanently.

Warren’s Architectural Heritage

Warren’s architectural heritage makes a community design process uniquely possible. The town has more architects per capita than almost anywhere in the US — a legacy of the 1960s Prickly Mountain design/build movement begun by David Sellers and Yale architecture graduates, which later spawned the Yestermorrow Design/Build School in Waitsfield. Engaging this community to reimagine the Town Hall space could transform a governance dispute into a design challenge the whole town can own. It already did this once — in 2009, when Vermont woodworkers built all of the library’s shelving by hand, and neighbors moved the collection book by book.
